ECONOMY
Los Angeles (LA) County’s job market experienced modest setbacks between
January 2024 and January 2025, with total nonfarm employment dipping by
13,900 jobs, a 0.3% year-over-year (YOY) decline. During the same period, the
county's unemployment rate inched up from 5.6% to 5.8%, although it remains 20
basis points (bps) below the quarterly average of 6.0%.¹ Some industries were hit
harder than others. Manufacturing saw the steepest decline, shedding 13,600 jobs
(-4.3% YOY). Wholesale trade was down by 4,700 jobs (-2.4% YOY) and retail
trade dropped 3,500 jobs (-0.9% YOY). In contrast, the transportation and
warehousing sector added 1,800 jobs, reflecting a modest 0.8% YOY increase.
Meanwhile, the ports of Los Angeles (1.7 million) and the Port of Long Beach (1.7
million), often seen as bellwethers of economic momentum, handled a combined
3.4 million Twenty-Foot Equivalent Units (TEUs) through February 2025, a 15.3%
YOY increase and an impressive 22.5% jump from pre-pandemic 2019 levels.²
This surge was driven by importers racing to get goods into the U.S. ahead of new
tariffs expected later this year. Also contributing to the increased activity over the
last few quarters were labor strikes at the East and Gulf Coast ports prompting
shippers to divert cargo to the West Coast; however, the International
Longshoremen’s Association (ILA) and the United States Maritime Alliance
(USMX) ratified a new six-year labor contract to avert potential future strikes.

Vacancy has been on the rise in LA’s industrial market. In Q1 2025, the overall
vacancy rate climbed to 4.9%, up 20 bps quarter-over-quarter (QOQ) and 140 bps
YOY, the highest level seen in the past decade. This marked the tenth consecutive
quarter of net occupancy losses. One of the most significant vacancies came from
National Road Logistics, which vacated 442,550 square feet (sf) in LA South. Of
the six major submarkets, LA South and Mid-Counties were the only submarkets
to report negative net absorption this quarter. On the flip side, some areas showed
signs of stability. LA Central and the San Gabriel Valley (SGV) posted slight QOQ
declines in vacancy rates, falling 10 bps and 30 bps, respectively. Still, the largest
increases were again in LA South and Mid-Counties, where vacancy rose by 90
bps and 80 bps, respectively, reaching 5.9% and 6.2%.

For perspective, LA’s overall industrial vacancy rate was under LGK0 just 11 quarters
ago; now, all six submarkets surpassed 3.0%, with three topping 5.5%.
Availability tells a similar story. The availability rate, which includes space being
marketed for lease even if not yet vacated, reached 6.5%, up 30 bps QOQ and 100
bps YOY. This increase suggests more vacancy may be on the horizon. At the end
of the first quarter, over 52.5 million square feet (msf) of industrial space was
available across LA, an increase of 2.5 msf QOQ and 8.7 msf YOY.
Still, there are bright spots. New leasing activity in Q1 2025 totaled nearly 8.0 msf, a
strong rebound with an 8.4% QOQ and 55.8% YOY jump. After a record-breaking
year in 2024, recording the highest annual leasing activity since 2021, 2025 is off to
a promising start. The bulk of new deals were inked in SGV, LA Central and LA
South, accounting for over 76.6% of LA’s total activity. SGV stood out, leading the
region in leasing volume for the third straight quarter and reaching its highest level
since Q4 2020.

OUTLOOK

FUTURE INVENTORY

While many U.S. markets grapple with excess supply, LA’s industrial construction
pipeline has remained modest, though it is beginning to taper. At the close of Q1
2025, 40 properties, totaling 4.9 msf, were under construction. Notably, all are being
developed on a speculative basis, and only one has been pre-leased, leaving 96.5%
of the space without tenant commitments thus far. Without a pickup in leasing, this
could further push vacancy rates upward. Additionally, 10 buildings totaling nearly
1.4 msf were completed this quarter, adding to the available inventory.

Despite the stronger leasing activity, the continued rise in the vacancy rate
continued to cool asking rents in LA. After the rapid rise during the COVID-19
pandemic and stagnating in the first half of 2023, the average asking rent has been
falling over the past six quarters. The overall asking rate dropped by $0.03 (2.1%
QOQ) and $0.19 (11.9% YOY) to $1.37 per square foot (psf) per month on a triple
net basis. Warehouse and distribution space yielded an even sharper dip, down by
$0.04 (3.0% QOQ) and $0.20 (12.9% YOY), also settling at $1.37 psf in Q1 2025.
Still, long-term growth tells a different story despite the recent softening of asking
rents. Compared to Q4 2019, average rents have swelled by 51.6%. Tenants exiting
leases signed three or more years ago are encountering significant rent spikes,
leading to sticker shock and difficult lease negotiations.
