ECONOMY

SPACE DEMAND / DELIVERIES

Between August 2024 and August 2025, Los Angeles (LA) County’s job market
added 31,600 nonfarm jobs, a 0.7% year-over-year (YOY) increase, marking
continued though modest growth in the regional labor market. Private education
and health services drove overall gains with 54,800 new jobs, followed by
government (+17,500; +3.1% YOY) and leisure and hospitality (+1,100). In
contrast, several industrial-related sectors contracted. Manufacturing (-11,000)
posted the largest decline, led by durable goods and transportation equipment,
while construction (-6,000) and trade, transportation and utilities (-5,400) also
softened, signaling a pullback in production and logistics activity tied to higher costs
and slower goods movement. Additional losses occurred in professional and
business services (-9,800) and financial activities (-4,900). The unemployment rate
improved to 6.3%, down from 6.7% a year earlier but still above the quarterly
average of 5.8%¹. Total employment is forecast to expand by 0.9% in 2025 and
0.7% in 2026, outpacing the five-year historical average of 0.0%², signaling modest
but sustained growth led by the healthcare and public sectors.

Vacancy rates are still on the rise in the LA industrial market but have been slowing
over the past year. In Q3 2025, the overall vacancy rate climbed to 4.8%, up 10
basis points (bps) quarter-over-quarter (QOQ) and 40 bps YOY, the highest level
seen in the past decade. The LA North submarket registered the largest quarterly
increase in vacancy, at 1.1%, while Mid-Counties registered the largest decrease at
1.0%. Year-to-date (YTD) net absorption is positive through three quarters for the
first time since 2022, as occupancy gains have been recorded in three of the past
four quarters.

The availability rate, which includes space being marketed for lease even if not
yet vacated, fell for the first time since Q4 2021 to 6.5%. This drop suggests a
shift in the vacancy momentum may be on the horizon. At the end of the third
quarter, nearly 52.6 million square feet (msf) of space was available across LA, a
decrease of nearly 1.1 msf QOQ. Sublease space accounted for 15.9% of the
total available space.

OUTLOOK

Leasing activity could be the brightest spot in the LA industrial market as over 9.7
msf of new leasing activity was recorded in Q3 2025 (+16.3% QOQ and +13.2%
YOY), marking the highest quarterly velocity since Q2 2021. YTD leasing in 2025
is over 26.7 msf, which is also the strongest through three quarters since 2021.
LA South led the way in Q3 2025 leasing followed by LA Central, which together
accounted for 58.4% of total quarterly leasing. Those two submarkets, along with
the San Gabriel Valley, represent 74.1% of total YTD leasing.

FUTURE INVENTORY

While many U.S. markets grapple with excess supply, LA’s industrial construction
pipeline has remained modest and tapered over the last year. At the close of Q3
2025, 23 properties, totaling over 4.0 msf, were under construction. Notably, all
are being developed on a speculative basis, and only one has been pre-leased,
leaving 98.7% of the space without tenant commitments thus far. Twelve
buildings, totaling over 1.5 msf, were completed this quarter, bringing the 2025
YTD deliveries to over 4.0 msf across 35 buildings. The bulk of LA’s development
is concentrated in LA North and LA South as those two submarkets are first and
second, respectively, in both under construction and YTD completions.

PRICING

Despite the more vigorous leasing activity, the continued rise in the vacancy rate
cooled asking rents in LA. After the rapid increase during the COVID-19
pandemic and stagnation in 2023, the average asking rent has been falling over
the past eight quarters. However, like the slowing of the vacancy rate growth, the
downward momentum of asking rates has begun to flatten out as well. The
overall asking rate dropped by $0.01 per square foot (psf) (-0.7%) QOQ and
$0.10 psf (-6.9%) YOY to $1.34 psf per month on a triple net basis.
Still, long-term growth tells a different story despite the recent softening of asking
rents. Compared to pre-COVID levels in Q4 2019, average rents have swelled by
48.5%. Tenants exiting leases signed five or more years ago are encountering
significant rent spikes, leading to sticker shock and complex lease negotiations.
