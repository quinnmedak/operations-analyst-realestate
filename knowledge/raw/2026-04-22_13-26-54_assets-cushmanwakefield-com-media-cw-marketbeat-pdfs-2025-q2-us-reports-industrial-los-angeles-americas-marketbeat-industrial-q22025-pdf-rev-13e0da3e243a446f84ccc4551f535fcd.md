ECONOMY

Los Angeles (LA) County’s job market recorded mild improvements between May
2024 and May 2025, with total nonfarm employment increasing by 21,800 jobs, a
0.5% year-over-year (YOY) increase. The unemployment rate remained
unchanged from April 2025 to May 2025, settling at 5.4% and is 40 basis points
1
(bps) below the quarterly average of 5.8%. Employment growth in private
education and health services continued to bolster the labor market. The sector
added 49,200 jobs (+5.1%) YOY, with healthcare and social assistance accounting
for most of the gains. The government sector also reported positive momentum,
expanding by 10,500 jobs (+1.8%) YOY, largely due to hiring at the local and state
levels. Industrial-using sectors, however, faced stronger headwinds. The
manufacturing sector recorded employment losses of 14,600 jobs (-4.7%) YOY.
The wholesale trade sector lost 4,400 jobs (-2.1%) YOY and retail lost 1,100 jobs (-
0.3%) YOY, yet the transportation and warehousing sector added 2,900 jobs
(+1.3%) YOY. All employment sectors in LA County are projected to grow at a
combined growth rate of 0.9% in 2025 and 0.7% in 2026 compared to the five-year

For perspective, LA’s overall industrial vacancy rate was under LGK0 just 12
quarters ago; now, five of six submarkets surpassed 3.0%, with two topping
6.0%.

Availability tells a similar story. The availability rate, which includes space being
marketed for lease even if not yet vacated, reached 6.8%, up 30 bps QOQ and
90 bps YOY. This increase suggests more vacancy may be on the horizon. At the
end of the second quarter, over 54.9 million square feet (msf) of industrial space
was available across LA, an increase of nearly 3.6 msf QOQ and 7.5 msf YOY.
Still, there are bright spots. New leasing activity in Q2 2025 totaled over 7.6 msf,
after recording nearly 8.6 msf in the first quarter. Year-to-date leasing in 2025 is
over 16.2 msf, which marks the highest velocity through the first half of a year
since 2021. The bulk of new deals were inked in SGV and LA Central, accounting
for 53.7% of LA’s total activity. SGV stood out, leading the region in leasing
volume for the fourth.

FUTURE INVENTORY

FUTURE INVENTORY
While many U.S. markets grapple with excess supply, LA’s industrial construction
pipeline has remained modest and tapered over the last year. At the close of Q2
2025, 33 properties, totaling 4.5 msf, were under construction. Notably, all are
being developed on a speculative basis, and only two have been pre-leased,
leaving 95.0% of the space without tenant commitments thus far. Without a
pickup in leasing, this could further push vacancy rates upward. Additionally, 11
buildings totaling 916,048 square feet (sf) were completed this quarter, adding to
the available inventory.

Despite the stronger leasing activity, the continued rise in the vacancy rate
continued to cool asking rents in LA. After the rapid increase during the COVID-
19 pandemic and stagnating in 2023, the average asking rent has been falling
over the past seven quarters. The overall asking rate dropped by $0.03 per
square foot (psf) (-2.4%) QOQ and $0.15 psf (-10.2%) YOY to $1.34 psf per
month on a triple net basis. Warehouse and distribution space yielded a similar
dip, down by $0.03 psf (-2.1%) QOQ and $0.17 psf (-10.9%) YOY, settling at
$1.35 psf in Q2 2025.
