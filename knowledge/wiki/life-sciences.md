# Life Sciences Real Estate

**Sources synthesized:** JLL 2025 Life Sciences Real Estate Perspective and Cluster Analysis (Sep 2025), JLL Lab Market Newsroom Release (Oct 2025)

> **Source note:** Both sources in this knowledge base are JLL publications. There is no CBRE or Cushman & Wakefield cross-validation for life sciences in this KB. JLL's analysis should be read as expert opinion from one of the sector's leading advisors, not as a consensus view across research firms.

---

## 1. Market Overview: A Tenant's Market

The U.S. life sciences real estate market is in a historically tenant-favorable position — the direct result of a post-pandemic construction boom that outpaced demand by a wide margin. As of mid-2025, 61 million square feet of lab/R&D space is available nationally, with an availability rate of approximately 29% — up 20.4 percentage points in just three years (JLL Life Sciences Perspective, Sep 2025).

That availability rate puts life sciences among the most oversupplied property sectors in the U.S. market, in sharp contrast to industrial (7.5% nationally) or even struggling office (which in some markets has not reached these levels). Tenants seeking lab space today have more choices, more leverage, and lower effective rents than at any point in the past decade. The pain is falling disproportionately on buildings delivered since early 2022 — the oversupply cohort that hit the market just as leasing momentum stalled.

---

## 2. Supply Correction: How We Got Here

The oversupply didn't develop through mismanagement at individual properties — it was a sector-wide dynamic. The 2020–2022 pandemic era flooded life sciences with venture capital, which drove demand for lab space. Developers responded by building at scale. But two things happened simultaneously: venture capital deployment slowed (biotech's share of VC dollars fell from ~15% historically to just 7% in recent quarters), and a new category of tenant — AI-native biotechs — began leasing far less space per company than traditional biotechs (JLL Life Sciences Perspective and Newsroom, 2025).

The result is a supply glut with no near-term demand shock large enough to absorb it quickly. Lab leases have declined by an average of 8% per year while venture funding has dropped 12% annually since the peak (JLL Life Sciences Perspective, Sep 2025). JLL projects the availability rate will decline from 29% to approximately 20% by 2030 — but the mechanism is supply rationalization, not demand recovery. Roughly 18.7 million SF of the current available stock is projected to shift to alternative uses through distress sales and adaptive reuse by the end of the decade (JLL Lab Market Newsroom, Oct 2025). Recovery will be gradual and uneven across markets.

---

## 3. Demand Shifts: AI and Reshoring Pulling in Opposite Directions

Two forces are reshaping life sciences demand, and they point in partially opposite directions.

**AI compression:** AI-native biotechs now account for one-sixth of all biotech VC deals and are growing rapidly — but they lease roughly one-third less space than traditional biotechs, with a lower lab-to-office ratio (45% lab to 55% office vs. traditional biopharma's ~60% lab share). As AI companies capture more of the growth in the sector, aggregate lab space demand per dollar of investment shrinks (JLL Life Sciences Perspective, Sep 2025).

**Pharma reshoring expansion:** Global pharmaceutical companies have pledged $475 billion year-to-date in U.S. manufacturing and R&D investments, driven by evolving federal trade and supply chain policy (JLL Lab Market Newsroom, Oct 2025). These companies are signing a growing share of midsize and large R&D leases — providing a demand floor that venture-backed leasing no longer provides. But their space preference skews toward manufacturing and established R&D hubs rather than the speculative urban lab clusters that drove the construction boom.

A third factor adds headwind: Chinese biotechs now account for four times the in-licensing deals with U.S. biopharma firms compared to 2021 — meaning risk capital is flowing toward Chinese IP rather than domestic lab demand. Post-licensing, 72% of those leases are office-heavy configurations, further suppressing lab absorption (JLL Lab Market Newsroom, Oct 2025).

## 4. LA as a Life Sciences Market

Los Angeles is not the top national life sciences market overall — Boston leads in comprehensive metrics (talent, employment growth, VC), followed by the San Francisco Bay Area and San Diego (JLL Lab Market Newsroom, Oct 2025). But LA holds a specific advantage that matters increasingly for where the sector is going: **LA ranks 3rd nationally in AI life sciences integration**, behind only the Bay Area and Boston, with a high concentration of AI-enabled life sciences companies, patent activity, and VC deployment in AI-bio convergence technologies.

That ranking matters because AI-native biotechs are the fastest-growing segment of the sector — and the markets best positioned for the next cycle of life sciences growth are those that can attract them. LA's proximity to San Diego (a major traditional biotech hub) and its entertainment-technology convergence culture give it structural advantages in this emerging segment.

What LA lacks from this knowledge base's sources is detailed submarket-level lab vacancy data or specific deal activity in LA's life sciences clusters (Century City, Torrance, El Segundo have emerged as LA life sciences nodes in practice). The LA-specific story exists but is not captured in these JLL national sources.

## 5. Investment Implications

The life sciences real estate investment story in 2025–2026 is primarily a distressed asset story. With 18.7M SF expected to shift to alternative uses by 2030, the question for investors is: which properties repurpose successfully, and at what price (JLL Life Sciences Perspective, Sep 2025)?

The strongest markets — Boston, Bay Area, San Diego, LA — will see selective absorption of quality lab product as pharma reshoring creates demand from established tenants. Weaker markets and suburban lab clusters that overbuilt speculatively face genuine obsolescence risk, with adaptive reuse (to office, multifamily, or mixed-use) as the most likely resolution.

For an LA-focused investor, the city's AI life sciences ranking suggests that well-located lab product in LA's emerging clusters could attract the new cohort of AI-native tenants even as traditional biotech demand remains soft. The caveat: those tenants lease smaller footprints, so per-SF metrics may recover before occupancy rates do.
