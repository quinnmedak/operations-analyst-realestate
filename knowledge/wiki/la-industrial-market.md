## Three-Quarter Trend: Absorbing the Correction

The most important thing about LA industrial in 2025 is not any single quarter's number — it is the direction of travel across three quarters of data. The market was in post-pandemic oversupply correction through most of 2025, but by Q3 it had turned the corner.

**Q1 2025 — Trough:** The vacancy rate hit 4.9%, the highest in more than a decade — for context, it was under 1% just 11 quarters prior, and by Q1 2025 all six submarkets had crossed 3.0% with three above 5.5%. This was the 10th consecutive quarter of net occupancy losses (-730K SF YTD). The headline is misleading: leasing volume surged 55.8% YOY to 8.0M SF in Q1, the strongest Q1 start since 2021. The divergence between rising vacancy and rising leasing reflects the lag between new leases signed and space physically occupied — tenants were committing but prior vacates still dominated the net. The single largest Q1 vacate: National Road Logistics returned 442,550 SF in LA South.

Port throughput was an outsized Q1 factor: the Port of LA and Long Beach combined handled 3.4M TEUs through February (+15.3% YOY, +22.5% vs. pre-pandemic 2019 levels), driven by importers racing to ship goods ahead of anticipated tariffs. This tariff frontloading pulled demand forward into Q1–Q2, creating a temporary surge that later reversed. The International Longshoremen's Association and USMX also ratified a new six-year labor contract in Q1, removing West Coast port strike risk as a near-term headwind for LA logistics users (Cushman LA Industrial Q1 2025).

A critical rent context that the vacancy numbers obscure: average asking rent fell 11.9% YOY to $1.37/SF NNN by Q1 2025, but rents are still 51.6% above Q4 2019 pre-pandemic levels. Tenants rolling off leases signed three or more years ago are encountering significant rent spikes — sticker shock that is slowing lease negotiations even as landlords lower current ask rates (Cushman LA Industrial Q1 2025).

**Q2 2025 — Stabilization:** YTD net absorption nearly broke even at -56K SF — effectively flat after a full year of losses. Leasing through H1 hit 16.2M SF, the highest H1 since 2021. Rents continued falling (-2.4% QOQ to $1.34/SF NNN) but Cushman's forecast acknowledged this: rents expected to fall ~0.9% for full year 2025 then stabilize and improve in 2026. The tariff frontloading effect was also visible — Port of LA/Long Beach handled 8.1M TEUs through May (+10.2% YOY), but May dropped 20.7% month-over-month as the tariff-driven surge pulled forward demand (Cushman LA Industrial Q2 2025).

**Q3 2025 — Recovery confirmed:** YTD net absorption flipped positive to +1.6M SF — the first positive YTD reading since 2022. The availability rate fell for the first time since Q4 2021 to 6.5% — note this matches the Q1 2025 level (6.5%), having peaked at 6.8% in Q2; the significance is the reversal, not an absolute low. Leasing hit 9.7M SF in the quarter alone (+16.3% QOQ, +13.2% YOY), the highest single-quarter velocity since Q2 2021. Cushman explicitly noted the downward rent momentum was flattening — "rents are still on the decline [but] expect rates to bottom out soon before improving" (Cushman LA Industrial Q3 2025). The signal that matters most: availability falling for the first time in four years is not coincidental with a single strong quarter — it reflects underlying demand absorbing the spec pipeline.

The full-year 2025 picture confirms the trajectory: total leasing reached ~33.3M SF, up from 29.7M in 2024, 24.3M in 2023, and 22.1M in 2022 (Cushman & Wakefield via Bisnow capital markets). Four consecutive years of rising leasing volume, even as vacancy was climbing.

## Submarket Breakdown

The LA industrial market's 800M+ SF of inventory is split across six submarkets with meaningfully different performance profiles:

| Submarket | Inventory (SF) | Vacancy (Q3) | YTD Net Absorption (Q3) | YTD Leasing (Q3) | Avg Rent (NNN) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| LA North | 149.7M | 4.4% | -959K SF | 2.8M SF | $1.42 |
| San Gabriel Valley | 159.6M | 3.0% | +2.2M SF | 6.6M SF | $1.30 |
| Mid-Counties | 89.5M | 5.0% | +756K SF | 4.0M SF | $1.31 |
| **LA Central** | 202.6M | 5.1% | +1.4M SF | 6.9M SF | $1.15 |
| LA West | 8.5M | 2.7% | +57K SF | 140K SF | $2.38 |
| **LA South** | 194.1M | 6.3% | -1.8M SF | 6.3M SF | $1.46 |

Source: Cushman & Wakefield LA Industrial Q3 2025

**SGV and LA Central are carrying the recovery.** SGV (159M SF) ran the highest leasing volume through three quarters (6.6M SF) and sits at 3.0% vacancy — the tightest large submarket in LA. E-commerce and import logistics dominate: Q1 2025 top deals include 694K SF to US Elogistics, 217K SF to HYTX Logistics (Cushman Q1). Q2's top deal was a 446K SF sublease by YS Express / Dreamway Logistics at 5500 S. Sheila St., Commerce — all 3PL/logistics names.

LA Central (202M SF — largest submarket) absorbed the Amazon effect. A single 615K SF lease in Commerce pushed the submarket into high positive net absorption in 2025 — a concrete example of how one large deal moves the needle in a market where space is measured in hundreds of millions of square feet (Bisnow capital markets). Central LA's YTD absorption of +1.4M SF through Q3 is almost entirely attributable to Amazon and similar scale tenants.

**LA South is the problem submarket.** At -1.8M SF YTD absorption through Q3, it offsets much of the SGV/Central LA recovery. LA South saw the most new speculative construction deliveries (504K SF Goodman Group project in Long Beach; 429K SF Brookfield in Carson), and the vacancy hit 6.3% — the highest of any submarket. The South Bay area within LA South is different: aerospace/defense and logistics users drove the most new leasing activity in the entire LA market (JLL Director Makenna Peter via Bisnow capital markets). The problem is spec deliveries in Long Beach and Compton absorbing less quickly than South Bay new leases.

**LA North** had the sharpest Q3 vacancy increase (+1.1% QOQ to 4.4%) driven by a wave of construction completions — Amazon-occupied facility in Simi Valley (496K SF), multiple Santa Clarita projects totaling 427K SF (Cushman Q3).

## Tenant Demand by Industry

Three demand profiles are driving LA industrial leasing, and they have different risk profiles:

**3PL / logistics consolidation** is the single largest demand driver nationally and locally. JLL's 13th Annual Industrial Tenant Demand Study (Aug 2025) found total industrial demand fell 10.9% YOY as occupiers delayed decisions — but within that decline, 3PL demand rose 12.8% YOY while traditional retailers cut requirements by 16.7% YOY. Manufacturing demand also rose while Warehouse & Distribution requirements declined, reflecting reshoring and defense spending as emerging structural demand sources. Nationally, the Southeast is the most active region (25%+ of total demand), led by Atlanta, Savannah, and Greenville/Spartanburg on the back of business-friendly policies and manufacturing resurgence — providing context for why LA must compete on location specificity (port access, aerospace/defense cluster) rather than cost. LA reflects this: SGV's consistent top-leasing-submarket status is driven by logistics and e-commerce distribution users (US Elogistics 694K, YiTong Investments 232K, HYTX Logistics 217K in Q1 alone). These users are consolidating into larger, more modern facilities — build-to-suit inquiries nationally up 117% since 2018 as tenants seek greater operational control (JLL Tenant Demand Study).

**Aerospace and defense** anchor the South Bay. This is LA-specific and durable — aerospace/defense companies lease industrial space near LAX and the ports for supply chain and logistics reasons that are largely insensitive to e-commerce trends or tariff cycles. JLL's Makenna Peter called South Bay "the most new leasing activity in the LA market" specifically citing aerospace/defense (Bisnow capital markets).

**E-commerce / big-box** remains the headline category nationally. Big-box leasing (500K SF+) surged 80.7% YOY in Q1 2026 nationally — the clearest signal that large logistics commitments are back after a cautious 2023–2024 (JLL US Industrial Q1 2026). Amazon's 615K SF Commerce deal is the LA manifestation of this national trend.

**Port-driven demand** is volatile but structural. LA/Long Beach handles more container volume than any other U.S. port complex. Through Q3 2025, combined TEUs were 13.5M YTD (+6.3% YOY, +19.9% above 2019 pre-pandemic levels) — but July's record 1M+ TEU month was followed by August deceleration as tariff frontloading pulled forward demand (Cushman Q3). This creates short-cycle volatility in the port submarkets (LA South) even as structural port demand remains anchored.

## Investment Activity

Q4 2025 brought $1.2B in SoCal industrial sales, +6% YOY (CBRE via Bisnow industrial). Private buyers drove 63.8% of acquisitions — up sharply from 46.1% in 2024 — while REIT share collapsed from 24.7% to 7.7%. The interpretation from CBRE's Michael Longo: private capital is "calling a bottom on the fundamentals" and acting before institutional buyers enter. Institutional buyers require a track record of returns; private buyers move on conviction (Bisnow industrial Q4 2025).

The quarter's standout deal: Morgan Stanley bought a 143K SF Amazon warehouse near LAX at $1,480/SF — well above the quarterly average of $312/SF. A fully-leased, single-tenant, port-adjacent Amazon warehouse commands a premium regardless of marketwide fundamentals, illustrating the bifurcation between core logistics assets and the broader market.

Owner-user buyers are also growing: 10.4% of LA/OC industrial buyers in 2025 vs. 8.8% in 2024, driven by companies wanting to lock in space control at below-peak valuations (Bisnow capital markets). This mirrors the office owner-user trend but from a different motivation — industrial users buying for operational certainty, office users buying for tax/financial advantages.

## National Context

LA industrial at 4.8% vacancy sits well below the national average of 7.5% (Q1 2026, JLL US Industrial). This is partly structural: LA's land-constrained, infill nature limits speculative development to no more than 1.2% of inventory under construction at any time (Cushman Q3 2025). No other major U.S. market has this constraint. The trade-off is higher rents at cycle peaks (LA hit >$2/SF NNN at peak 2022) and deeper corrections when demand normalizes — because there is no valve to release excess demand into affordable peripheral land.

Nationally, industrial leasing rallied to 145.2M SF in Q1 2026 (+17.8% YOY) with net absorption of 50.9M SF — demonstrating the sector is back in growth mode after a cautious 2023–2024 (JLL US Industrial Q1 2026). JLL's Property Clock places LA in the "Bottoming out" phase for industrial in Q4 2025 (JLL Global Perspective) — same as the office market, consistent with the three-quarter trend data showing stabilization but not yet accelerating recovery.

## Market Narrative

LA industrial's three-quarter 2025 trajectory tells a recovery story that is more credible than most pundits acknowledged at mid-year. The market absorbed the post-pandemic vacancy surge, flipped positive YTD absorption for the first time since 2022, and posted record quarterly leasing velocity in Q3 — all while national industrial fundamentals were also strengthening.

The risk is LA South: persistent over-delivery of spec space in Long Beach/Compton is the one structural headwind that the SGV and Central LA recovery has not yet offset. Whether LA South stabilizes in 2026 depends on whether port throughput normalizes after tariff-related volatility and whether the spec pipeline tapers (construction under construction fell from 4.9M SF in Q1 to 4.0M SF by Q3 — a positive sign). Q4 2025 absorption reverting to -116K SF suggests the Q3 reading was not yet a durable turn; the construction pipeline taper is the more reliable leading indicator to watch than any single quarter's absorption figure.

Q4 2025 snapped back to negative absorption (-116K SF per CBRE via Bisnow) — but notably it was the lowest negative quarter in two years, suggesting the market is oscillating around the bottom rather than resuming a downward trend. CBRE's Michael Longo framed LA's unique position directly: "We're so port-focused, and we had the tariff shock to the system. We're still recovering from that increase in vacancy we saw coming out of the pandemic. No other market had that." (Bisnow, Jan 2026). For context, the Inland Empire — LA's closest peer market — ended 2025 at 7.1% vacancy, 220 bps above LA's Q3 reading, confirming LA industrial's relative supply constraint even in the correction.

Where sources agree: Cushman's three quarters of data and Bisnow's Q4 investment activity both point to bottoming fundamentals and recovering investor conviction. The Bisnow/CBRE "calling a bottom" characterization aligns with Cushman's explicit Q3 forecast language about rents "bottoming out soon."

## Q1 2026 Full Report (CBRE)

CBRE's Q1 2026 LA Industrial Figures confirm the inflection: net absorption turned positive for the first time since 2022 at +934,025 SF, reversing from -1.9M SF in Q1 2025. Vacancy rose to 5.4% (availability 8.1%) even as absorption turned positive — reflecting a pipeline of newly delivered space. The headline is real but carries a caveat: the Trader Joe's ~1.0M SF distribution center in Palmdale delivered and occupied in Q1. Without that single deal, net absorption would have been slightly negative.

### Submarket Performance (Q1 2026, CBRE definitions)

CBRE uses five submarkets (vs. Cushman's six). Submarket definitions are not identical; cross-source submarket comparisons require care.

| Submarket | NRA (MSF) | Vacancy | Availability | Avg Rent ($/SF NNN) | QTR Absorption |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Central Los Angeles | 243.3 | 5.3% | 8.0% | $0.99 | +257,000 SF |
| Greater San Fernando Valley | 172.6 | 4.8% | 6.8% | $1.34 | +590,000 SF |
| Mid-Counties | 102.4 | 5.8% | 9.1% | $1.26 | -320,000 SF |
| **South Bay** | 199.0 | 6.3% | 9.5% | **$1.36** | **+643,000 SF** |
| San Gabriel Valley | 157.8 | 5.0% | 7.3% | $1.22 | -235,000 SF |
| **LA Total** | **875.2** | **5.4%** | **8.1%** | **$1.21** | **+934,000 SF** |

Source: CBRE Research, Q1 2026

South Bay led absorption (+643K SF) driven by the Anduril 1.185M SF new lease in Long Beach — the largest deal of the quarter. The Greater San Fernando Valley (+590K SF) was the other major driver, benefiting from the Trader Joe's Palmdale delivery. Mid-Counties (-320K SF) and San Gabriel Valley (-235K SF) remained in negative territory.

### Rent Decline: 30.5% from Peak

Asking rent reached $1.21/SF NNN in Q1 2026, down 1.6% QOQ from $1.23 and 8.3% YOY from $1.32. The cumulative decline from Q1 2023's peak of $1.74/SF is 30.5%. Submarket spread: South Bay highest at $1.36/SF, Central LA lowest at $0.99/SF — a $0.37 spread across the five submarkets.

### Availability: Doubled in Three Years

Overall availability closed Q1 2026 at 8.1%, up from 4.0% in Q1 2023. This more than doubling reflects both new spec deliveries and a large volume of sublease space (sublease availability: 1.2% = ~10.5M SF). South Bay has the highest sublease rate at 1.6%.

### Construction: Developers Pulling Back

- Q1 2026 deliveries: 1.57M SF (more than double the 0.7M SF a year earlier)
- Pipeline: 4.53M SF under construction — down 19.1% QOQ from 5.6M SF and 34.5% YOY from 6.9M SF
- Greater San Fernando Valley has the largest pipeline share (1.16M SF) and delivered 1.22M SF in Q1 alone

The pipeline contraction is the most important leading indicator: developers have been pulling back for four consecutive quarters. Less new supply coming means absorption will more reliably translate to vacancy reduction in 2026–2027.

### Leasing Velocity: Strongest in Three Years

14.6M SF total gross leasing activity in Q1 2026, up 27.3% QOQ and 4.4% YOY — the highest quarterly velocity in the prior three years. South Bay led with 37.4% (5.5M SF), driven by advanced manufacturing and defense users. Advanced manufacturing accounted for five of the top ten leases.

### Key Q1 2026 Transactions

| Tenant | SF | Type | Submarket |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anduril | 1,185,000 | New Lease | South Bay (Long Beach) |
| Apex Logistics | 443,000 | New Lease | South Bay (Torrance) |
| SpeedX | 327,000 | New Lease | Commerce |
| Neros | 252,000 | Sublease | South Bay (Torrance) |

Anduril — a defense technology company — anchors the aerospace/defense demand story in South Bay. This is the largest deal in the LA market in Q1 2026 and illustrates the shift in demand mix from pure e-commerce/logistics toward advanced manufacturing and defense.

### Port Context

San Pedro Bay (Ports of LA and Long Beach) handled 20M+ TEUs in 2025 (+0.9% YOY). Port of LA: 10.2M TEUs (3rd busiest calendar year in its 118-year history). Port of Long Beach: 9.9M TEUs (all-time record). Port throughput remains the structural demand anchor for South Bay and Central LA industrial.
