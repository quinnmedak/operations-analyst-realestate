## Demand Drivers & Tenant Activity

Two industries dominate LA office leasing, and they have opposite profiles:

**Financial services** led Q2 2025 leasing volume with 584,207 SF (Cushman Q2 2025). Oaktree Capital's 220K SF new lease at 555 S. Flower is the clearest signal: institutional financial firms — private equity, asset managers — are still committing to in-person, full-floor office in the Financial District despite DTLA's high vacancy. This is not a broad-market recovery signal; it is concentrated in a specific user profile that has maintained in-office culture.

**Legal services** contributed 287,192 SF, reinforcing the same pattern. Finance and law are among the few industries where in-person client interactions and confidentiality requirements sustain traditional office culture (Cushman Q2 2025).

Outside these two sectors, activity is renewal-driven rather than growth-driven. Princess Cruises' 138K SF renewal in Valencia/Newhall, Universal Music Group's 74K SF recommitment at Warner Center, and Amerihome Mortgage's 81K SF renewal in Westlake Village all represent tenants staying put rather than expanding. This is characteristic of a market where occupiers want optionality — they will renew rather than relocate, but won't take more space.

The employment backdrop confirms the weak demand story: LA County's office-using sectors shed jobs in the year ending May 2025, with financial activities down 4,700 jobs (-2.3%) and professional and business services down 4,100 jobs (-0.6%) YOY (Cushman Q2 2025). Fewer office-using employees means less space demanded.

## Investment & Capital Markets

Three deals define the current LA office investment thesis: distressed pricing, value-add repositioning, and owner-user acquisitions.

**Sunset Media Center** — 326K SF at 6255 Sunset Blvd. in Hollywood sold for $61M to Barker Pacific Group / Hankey Investment Co. at approximately 51% occupancy. The buyers framed it explicitly as buying "at a fraction of replacement cost" with upside through lease-up (Bisnow Sunset Media Center). At ~$187/SF, this is well below any reasonable construction cost for a 22-story Hollywood asset.

**601 S. Figueroa (Figueroa at Wilshire)** — The 52-story DTLA tower sold for $210M to Uncommon Developers after Brookfield's financial stress. The buyer is repositioning. This is capital returning to DTLA not because fundamentals are strong but because reset pricing makes value-add math work (Cushman Q2 2025).

**Owner-users** represent 23% of LA office sales in 2025, the highest share in years. Riot Games bought its Element LA headquarters from Hudson Pacific for $150M plus $81M to terminate its lease — the math on not paying rent and claiming tax shelter advantages is compelling when purchase prices are at current discounts (Bisnow capital markets). JLL's Jeffrey Bramson was working on three additional owner-user transactions totaling ~$400M at time of publication.

**Private capital** made up 61.4% of LA buyers in 2025, the highest in a decade, while institutional buyers reduced activity to 23.8% (MSCI via Bisnow capital markets). Institutional caution is explicit: they need a "couple years of good returns" before allocating, per MSCI's Jim Costello.

## Market Narrative

The LA office market sits at a floor-finding phase — not recovering, but showing credible signals that the worst is behind it.

The headline 24.1% vacancy and 12 consecutive quarters of negative net absorption are hard facts. So is the Q2 2025 leasing collapse (-50% QOQ). But Q2 was a single bad quarter within a year that logged 14M+ SF of total leasing — a post-pandemic high nationally. Q4 2025 availability fell 60 basis points to 27.6% (Savills via Bisnow). JLL's Property Clock places LA in the "Bottoming out" phase for office in Q4 2025 (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026) — same phase as San Francisco and Chicago, behind New York and Dallas which are already "growth accelerating."

The two most important structural forces:

**Trophy/commodity bifurcation is widening.** 1950 Avenue of the Stars in Century City (825K SF, 90%+ pre-leased) and DTLA at 32% vacancy are both the "LA office market" but they are experiencing different realities. The construction pipeline confirms this: 2.1M SF under construction, none of it in DTLA, heavily pre-leased in prime submarkets (Cushman Q2 2025). Where sources agree: JLL, Cushman, and Bisnow all describe the same bifurcation — demand is concentrating in best-in-class space.

**Distressed pricing is creating a buyer market.** Sellers — especially institutional owners like Kilroy Realty and Brookfield — are exiting at reset pricing. Private capital and owner-users are absorbing these assets. This is not a vote of confidence in the market recovery; it is a bet that current pricing overcompensates for the downside. Whether that bet pays off depends on whether LA office leasing sustains the 14M SF/year pace or reverts to the Q2 trough pattern.

Where sources differ: Q2 2025 Cushman data shows the market still losing occupancy; Bisnow's full-year figure and Q4 availability data suggest H2 2025 recovery. These are not contradictory — they reflect a market that bottomed mid-year and partially recovered. Neither source projects a sharp recovery.

## National Context

| Market | Vacancy (Q4 2025) | Property Clock Phase | Trend |
|---|---|---|---|
| Los Angeles | ~27.6% (availability) | Bottoming out | Partial H2 recovery |
| San Francisco | 34.4% | Bottoming out | More severe, similar phase |
| New York | 14.1% | Growth accelerating | Already recovering |
| Dallas | 25.7% | Growth accelerating | Recovering faster than LA |
| Washington DC | 23.0% | Bottoming out | Similar to LA |

Sources: JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026 (vacancy and clock), Bisnow (LA availability)

LA's position relative to peers: better than San Francisco (whose 34.4% vacancy reflects tech industry contraction more severe than LA's diversified base), worse than New York (where financial services employment anchors demand), and roughly in line with Dallas (higher vacancy but in growth-accelerating phase due to strong population/employment inflows). 

Nationally, JLL US Office Q1 2026 shows positive net absorption for the third consecutive quarter (+3.5M SF in Q1 2026), driven by New York (+1.5M SF) and San Francisco (+1.6M SF). LA is not yet contributing to national positive absorption, but the full-year 2025 leasing high suggests it is not falling further behind.
