## What Is JLL

JLL (Jones Lang LaSalle, NYSE: JLL) is a Fortune 500 commercial real estate services and investment management firm with annual revenue of $23.4 billion, 112,000+ employees, and operations in over 80 countries. Founded over 200 years ago, JLL helps clients buy, build, occupy, manage, and invest in commercial, industrial, hotel, residential, and retail properties globally (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026).

JLL's core service lines are:
- **Leasing** — tenant and landlord representation across all property types
- **Capital Markets** — investment sales, debt advisory, equity placement
- **Property & Facility Management** — day-to-day operations for owners and occupiers
- **Research & Consulting** — market data, forecasting, and advisory (the source of all JLL reports in this knowledge base)
- **Project & Development Services** — construction management and workplace strategy

The role this knowledge base was built for — Business Intelligence Analyst, JLL Rosemead, CA — sits within JLL's research and analytics function, supporting the leasing and capital markets teams with market intelligence and data.

## JLL's Research Framework

JLL Research publishes quarterly market reports for every major property sector and city, plus periodic global perspectives. Key publications in this knowledge base:

- **JLL US Office Market Dynamics** (quarterly) — national office fundamentals, net absorption, vacancy, rent trends
- **JLL US Industrial Market Dynamics** (quarterly) — national industrial leasing, vacancy, big-box trends
- **JLL Global Real Estate Perspective** (periodic) — cross-market comparison, Property Clock, global investment volumes

The **JLL Global Property Clock** is the firm's signature framework for placing markets in the real estate cycle. Four phases:
1. **Growth accelerating** — vacancy falling, rents rising, strong demand
2. **Rental growth slowing** — market tightening but momentum easing
3. **Rents falling** — oversupply, rising vacancy
4. **Bottoming out** — vacancy near peak, demand beginning to stabilize

As of Q4 2025, LA is positioned in **"Bottoming out"** for both office and industrial — the same phase as Boston, San Francisco, Chicago, and Washington DC. Markets in "Growth accelerating" include Dallas, New York, Tokyo, Dubai, and London (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026). The implication for LA: the worst of the cycle is behind it, but rental growth and deal volume recovery are still ahead.

## The Global CRE Market in 2026

JLL's February 2026 perspective identifies three headline trends shaping global real estate:

**Office leasing at a post-pandemic high.** Global office leasing rose 5% in 2025 to its highest level since 2019. US groundbreakings are at record lows, and three-quarters of the remaining construction pipeline is already pre-leased — meaning available high-quality space is becoming genuinely scarce. JLL data confirms this nationally: US office net absorption was positive for the third consecutive quarter in Q1 2026, with 3.5M SF of occupancy gains led by San Francisco (+1.6M SF) and New York (+1.5M SF). LA has not yet contributed to national positive absorption but its full-year 2025 leasing hit a post-pandemic high (JLL US Office Q1 2026).

**Capital markets momentum building.** Global direct investment volumes rose 19% from 2024 in Q4 2025, with the Americas up 14% YoY. Cross-border investment grew 25% for full-year 2025. CBRE projects US CRE investment will reach $562B in 2026 — a 16% increase — nearly matching the pre-pandemic 2015–2019 annual average (CBRE US Outlook 2026). The recovery is broadening: not just core markets, but value-add and opportunistic capital is moving again.

**Logistics rebounding.** Industrial leasing demand continued recovering from H1 2025 tariff uncertainty. US industrial leasing grew 17.8% YoY in Q1 2026, with big-box leasing (500K+ SF spaces) surging 80.7% YoY — a signal that large occupiers are making long-term commitments again (JLL US Industrial Q1 2026).

## LA Within JLL's Framework

JLL's Property Clock places LA in "Bottoming out" for both office and industrial as of Q4 2025 — which tells you where LA sits relative to the cycle, not whether it's a good or bad market in absolute terms.

For **office**: LA's 24.1% vacancy (Q2 2025, Cushman & Wakefield) is better than San Francisco (34.4%) but worse than New York (14.1%) and Dallas (25.7%). LA lags behind New York and Dallas in the recovery sequence — both are already in "Growth accelerating." The practical implication: tenants still have significant leverage in most LA submarkets, but trophy assets in LA West and Century City are already experiencing the supply constraints JLL identifies nationally.

For **industrial**: LA's vacancy (~6–7% range through 2025) is roughly in line with the national 7.5% rate (JLL US Industrial Q1 2026). The South Bay and Central LA submarkets have absorbed major new leases (Amazon's 615K SF Commerce deal), consistent with JLL's national trend of big-box demand recovery.
