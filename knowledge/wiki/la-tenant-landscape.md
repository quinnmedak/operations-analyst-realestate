# LA Tenant Landscape

**Last updated:** May 2026
**Primary sources:** Cushman & Wakefield LA Office MarketBeat Q2 2025, Bisnow (Sunset Media Center, Canvas Worldwide El Segundo, Reframe Studios)

---

## Overview

The LA office and industrial tenant base is more geographically fragmented than any other major US market — finance and law in DTLA, media and entertainment in Hollywood and the Westside, aerospace and logistics in the South Bay, and a growing cluster of media/marketing agencies in El Segundo. Each submarket has a distinct tenant profile, and understanding which industries go where is the core of underwriting any LA lease or acquisition. This page synthesizes tenant-level deal data to map who is leasing what, where, and why.

---

## DTLA: Finance and Legal Hold the Line

Downtown Los Angeles is the worst-performing LA office submarket by vacancy (32%+ across CBD and Non-CBD combined as of Q2 2025), but it remains the primary destination for two specific tenant categories: financial services and legal services.

**Financial services** led all Q2 2025 leasing volume citywide with 584,207 SF, anchored by Oaktree Capital's 220,000 SF new lease at 555 S. Flower in the Financial District — the single largest deal of the quarter (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025). Oaktree's commitment is significant not because DTLA is healthy, but because it confirms that institutional financial firms — private equity, asset managers — still value in-person, full-floor office culture in the Financial District even at current market conditions. This is a concentrated signal, not a broad recovery indicator.

**Legal services** contributed 287,192 SF of Q2 2025 leasing, the second-largest industry (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025). Finance and law share a structural reason for staying in DTLA: both industries depend on in-person client interactions, confidentiality requirements, and proximity to courts and financial institutions. Their in-office culture predates the pandemic and has been more durable than the broader market.

Outside these two sectors, DTLA leasing is renewal-driven. Tenants are staying put rather than expanding. This is the market's natural floor: firms with established DTLA presences are not leaving, but very few new-to-market or expansion tenants are taking DTLA space.

## Hollywood: Entertainment-Adjacent, Under Stress

Hollywood's office market sits at 31.3% availability — above the 27.6% marketwide rate (Bisnow Sunset Media Center, Jan 2026). The anchor tenant category is entertainment-adjacent: organizations that orbit the entertainment industry rather than studios themselves.

The Sunset Media Center sale illustrates the tenant profile: its occupants at the time of sale included the Hollywood Chamber of Commerce, OpenTable, and AIDS Healthcare Foundation — a civic organization, a tech/hospitality services company, and a healthcare nonprofit (Bisnow Sunset Media Center). None are traditional entertainment studios. Hollywood's CRE appeal is its brand and adjacency to the entertainment ecosystem, not direct studio occupancy.

Traditional entertainment companies — streamers, studios, production companies — are becoming increasingly selective about space. The 2023 writers' and actors' strikes accelerated restructuring of how entertainment firms use physical offices. Big-budget productions compete with streaming content and short-form social media, and the space requirements for each differ dramatically (Bisnow Reframe Studios). The Reframe Studios adaptive reuse in Atwater Village captures this shift: Swift Real Estate Partners converted a 7.3-acre industrial site (formerly Phillips Electronics) into two 23,000 SF soundstages plus 100,000 SF of office and support space — purpose-built to serve both traditional TV/film productions and new media content creators who need flexible, shorter-term arrangements. Industrial-to-studio conversion is an emerging LA-specific adaptive reuse trend, distinct from the office-to-residential story (Bisnow Reframe Studios).

The investment thesis for Hollywood office follows directly from this tenant picture: assets like Sunset Media Center attract value-add capital at distressed pricing because lease-up is possible, but only with a clear-eyed view of which tenant categories are actually active in the market.

## El Segundo and South Bay: Media, Professional Services, Aerospace

El Segundo is the standout submarket for understanding which tenants are actually expanding (not just renewing) in LA's office market. The submarket recorded an availability rate of 23% in Q2 2025 — below the marketwide 28.1% — and has attracted a steady stream of larger leases since early 2025 (Bisnow El Segundo).

Three deals define the El Segundo tenant profile:
- **Canvas Worldwide** — media and marketing agency, doubled its space to 68,000 SF at 2330 Utah Ave. (Frank Gehry-designed campus), relocating from ~36,000 SF in Playa Vista. Clients include Hyundai, Kia, Amazon MGM Studios, McDonald's, and Zillow. Represented by JLL's Jason Fine and Blake Searles (Bisnow El Segundo).
- **KPMG** — professional services, 50,000 SF at Plaza at Continental Park (Bisnow El Segundo).
- **The Toy Association** — trade/industry association, 95,000 SF at 101 Continental (Bisnow El Segundo).

What unites these tenants is proximity advantage: El Segundo sits adjacent to LAX, close to entertainment studios in Culver City and Manhattan Beach, and near the South Bay's aerospace and defense cluster. Media and marketing agencies like Canvas Worldwide benefit from proximity to both entertainment clients and the aerospace/tech employers that concentrate in the submarket. Canvas specifically moved from Playa Vista — not a step down in quality — which signals that El Segundo's product and submarket fundamentals justified the relocation.

The aerospace and logistics tenant base underpinning South Bay industrial demand is a separate story from the office market, but both stem from the same geographic advantage: LAX access, port proximity, and the legacy defense/aerospace employment base in the South Bay and Torrance.

## Suburban Renewals: Stable but Not Growing

The suburban LA office market — Valley, South Bay, and San Fernando/Santa Clarita — is characterized by large tenant renewals rather than new deals or expansions. Three Q2 2025 renewals illustrate the profile:

- **Princess Cruises** — 138,973 SF renewal at 24200 Magic Mountain Pkwy., Valencia/Newhall (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025)
- **Amerihome Mortgage** — 80,671 SF renewal at 1 Baxter Way, Westlake Village (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025)
- **Universal Music Group** — 74,354 SF renewal at 21301 Burbank Blvd., Warner Center (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025)
- **Univision Television** — 74,046 SF renewal at 5999 Center Dr., Culver City (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025)

The pattern is consistent: established companies with large suburban footprints are staying in place. They are not expanding their space footprint, but they are also not vacating. This represents the floor of suburban demand — occupancy sustained by inertia and switching costs rather than growth. The implication for investors and landlords: suburban renewals at below-market rents are the realistic outcome in the near term; meaningful absorption requires new-to-market or expansion tenants, which are rare outside El Segundo.

## What the Tenant Landscape Tells Investors and Brokers

Three practical conclusions emerge from synthesizing tenant activity across LA submarkets:

**Finance and law anchor DTLA's floor.** These industries will not vacate at scale. The investment thesis for distressed DTLA assets depends on this anchor holding while lease-up of vacant floors proceeds — not on a broad market recovery.

**El Segundo is the one submarket with genuine expansion demand.** Canvas Worldwide doubled its space. KPMG and The Toy Association signed new leases. The submarket's availability rate is below the marketwide average. For brokers, this is where tenant rep activity is most active on the demand side. For investors, this is where absorption is credible rather than aspirational.

**Entertainment tenants need production space more than traditional office.** The Reframe Studios conversion from industrial to soundstage represents a structural shift in how entertainment companies occupy space — flexible, purpose-built, multi-use — not a traditional office story. Brokers advising entertainment clients need to understand this distinction.
