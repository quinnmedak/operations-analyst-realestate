# Capital Markets & Investment

**Last updated:** April 2026  
**Primary sources:** CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook, CBRE Cap Rate Survey H1 2025, CBRE Cap Rate Survey H2 2025, Bisnow LA capital markets, JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026

---

## Cap Rate Environment

**Direction of travel:** Cap rates peaked in 2023–2024 and are now compressing. CBRE's H1 2025 survey of 200+ capital markets professionals found the all-property cap rate declined 9 basis points — the first decline in multiple years — despite highly volatile Treasury yields (10-year peaked at ~4.8% in January, fell to ~4.3% by March, then spiked again after April tariff announcements before settling around 4.2% by June). The conclusion from CBRE in H1 2025: "we are past the peak of cap rates and entering a new period of property yield compression" (CBRE Cap Rate Survey H1 2025).

By H2 2025, cap rates held steady across all sectors while the broader market picture improved significantly: transaction volume up ~19%, pricing stabilized with price indices "no longer falling," and the CBRE Lending Momentum Index moved "well above the prior five-year average" — meaning debt is more available, at higher LTVs, from more lenders (CBRE Cap Rate Survey H2 2025). The H2 survey found "nearly all respondents believe cap rates have peaked," with opinions split roughly equally between expecting decreases and no change over the next six months.

**Note on specific cap rate levels:** The actual cap rate ranges by sector and market (e.g., "stabilized industrial in LA: 4.5–5.5%") are published in the CBRE surveys as Infogram charts that did not extract from scraping. The directional data above is from the surveys' narrative sections. For specific current rate ranges by property type and submarket, consult the CBRE Cap Rate Survey PDF directly.

**2026 projection:** CBRE forecasts cap rates for most property types will compress 5–15 bps in 2026. "Good-quality assets are expected to see greater cap rate compression" — meaning the spread between prime and secondary assets will widen further even as headline averages compress (CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook).

**Sector sentiment:** CBRE H2 2025 respondents rank long-term sector investment performance: multifamily (1st) → industrial (2nd) → retail (3rd) → hotel (4th) → office (5th). However, office's ranking improved for the first time in years — 14% of respondents now believe office will have the best 10-year performance, up from near-zero in prior surveys. Retail and multifamily are seen as most "appropriately priced" given risk/income growth potential (CBRE Cap Rate Survey H2 2025).

## LA Capital Markets Recovery

LA commercial real estate sales reached $37.3B in 2025, up from $33B in 2024 and $35B in 2023 — but still well below the $51.4B recorded in 2019 (MSCI via Bisnow capital markets). This is recovery, not full rebound. Colliers Vice Chair Sean Fulp explicitly cautioned against using 2019 as the benchmark: "Commercial real estate as a whole has changed considerably — how people use space, what remote work has done to the office market, how AI is reshaping how people use real estate."

Two factors drove 2025's recovery in LA: interest rates fell, making debt serviceable again, and seller expectations reset to meet where buyers were willing to transact. Fulp: "The market had gotten comfortable and found a way to make sense of real estate values based upon flattening fundamentals and also flattening capital markets" (Bisnow capital markets). In other words: buyers and sellers finally agreed on what things were worth.

**Who is buying in LA:**

| Buyer type | 2025 share | Change from 2024 |
|---|---|---|
| Private capital | 61.4% | Up significantly (highest in a decade) |
| Institutional | 23.8% | Slightly down from 24.4% |
| Owner-users (office) | 23% of office sales | Up sharply |
| Owner-users (industrial) | 10.4% | Up from 8.8% |
| Cross-border | ~5% | Flat 2024–2025, dried up YTD 2026 |

Source: MSCI via Bisnow capital markets; JLL (owner-user figures)

Private capital moved first and hardest — 61.4% of buyers, highest in a decade, while private capital was only 51.2% of sellers. Private capital is net buying. Institutional capital is waiting for a track record of returns before allocating, per MSCI Research Executive Director Jim Costello: "Until people see a good track record of a couple years of good returns, they just don't want to allocate more." This is the classic distressed-market recovery pattern: private bets on the bottom, institutional validates after the fact.

Cross-border buying dried up YTD 2026 — not necessarily a political reaction, per Costello, but a "risk reaction" to system uncertainty: "It's harder to underwrite something when you don't know what the rules of the game are going to be in two weeks, let alone six months."

## National Investment Activity

CBRE projects $562B in US CRE investment in 2026, a 16% increase — nearly matching the 2015–2019 pre-pandemic annual average (CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook). The underlying demand signal is strong: in 2025 CBRE executed its most confidentiality agreements with prospective buyers since 2022, and new listings approached levels also not seen since 2022. Both metrics indicate deal pipelines building.

JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026 confirms the trend: global direct investment volumes up 19% in 2025 from 2024, with cross-border investment up 25% YOY for the full year and Americas up 14% YOY in Q4. The cross-border figure is globally significant but diverges from LA specifically, where cross-border has stalled (Bisnow).

CBRE's framing is important: "U.S. commercial real estate is affordable" relative to other asset classes. With benchmark rates expected to remain around 4%, total returns will be income-driven — not appreciation-driven. This sets the expectation: investors buying today are buying for cash flow, not for the cap rate compression trade. Appreciation upside is there if rates fall further, but the base case is income.

## Buyer/Seller Dynamics

**Private capital dominance** is the defining feature of the current cycle. In LA, 61.4% of buyers are private capital (Bisnow). Nationally, CBRE notes "sizable amounts of dry powder from both public and private investors" — the weight of capital is described as "an upside risk to our forecasts," meaning if private capital deploys faster than expected, transaction volume could exceed the 16% growth projection (CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook).

**Institutional waiting:** CBRE notes institutional investors "continue to broaden their targets" with retail and office expected to see "notable volume growth in 2026" — but the timing depends on seeing early-movers' returns. The LA data confirms this: 23.8% institutional share is below historic norms.

**Owner-users** are a specific LA phenomenon worth calling out. In office, 23% of LA sales were to owner-users in 2025 — the highest in years. JLL's Jeffrey Bramson cited tax shelter advantages and freedom from rent obligations as the drivers. Riot Games buying its Element LA headquarters for $150M + $81M lease termination is the headline example. Bramson was working on three additional owner-user transactions totaling ~$400M at time of publication (Bisnow). In industrial, the 10.4% owner-user share (up from 8.8%) reflects companies locking in control of space at below-peak pricing.

**Debt market:** CBRE expects banks to re-emerge as lenders in 2026 as the yield curve steepens, adding to the alternative lenders already active. Lending spreads expected to remain tight. Many maturing loans will be extended to 2027 and beyond rather than forced into distressed sales — a deliberate extension strategy that limits distressed volume but also delays the price discovery that would attract more buyers (CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook).

## Investment Themes in LA

**Value-add at reset pricing** is the dominant thesis, expressed in three deals:
- **Sunset Media Center** (Hollywood): $61M for 326K SF at 51% occupancy — ~$187/SF, explicitly bought "at a fraction of replacement cost" with lease-up upside (Bisnow)
- **601 S. Figueroa** (DTLA): $210M by Uncommon Developers after Brookfield's financial stress — 52-story repositioning play (Cushman LA Office Q2 2025)
- **Morgan Stanley / Amazon warehouse** (LAX): $211.4M for 143K SF ($1,480/SF) — fully-leased, port-adjacent, single-tenant industrial commands full pricing even in a soft market (Bisnow industrial Q4 2025)

The divergence between Sunset Media Center at $187/SF and the Amazon warehouse at $1,480/SF illustrates the same principle operating in opposite directions: asset quality and occupancy are the entire story. A 51%-occupied Hollywood office and a 100%-occupied Amazon logistics facility near LAX are both "LA real estate" but they are completely different investment propositions.

**Flight to quality** is where institutional capital is expected to return first. CBRE: "We expect investor interest will broaden to include well-located Class A [office] properties as spillover demand from prime trophy assets occurs" (CBRE Capital Markets 2026 Outlook). This means the Westside premium submarkets (Century City, Culver City, West LA) are the entry point for institutional office capital returning to LA.
