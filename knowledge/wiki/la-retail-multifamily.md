## LA Retail Market (Q2 2025)

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Vacancy rate | 5.8% |
| YTD net absorption | -879,255 SF |
| 12-month net absorption | -2.1M SF |
| Asking rent | $36.51 PSF |
| Rent growth Y-o-Y | -0.7% |
| Stage in rental cycle | Rents falling |
| Construction pipeline | 736,401 SF (0.1% of inventory, mostly pre-leased) |

Source: JLL LA Retail Market Dynamics Q2 2025 (PDF)

The headline numbers tell a contradictory story that requires unpacking. Absorption is negative — -879K SF YTD, -2.1M SF over 12 months — driven by consumer behavior shifts and store closures. Yet vacancy sits at just 5.8%, a figure that looks healthy relative to the 24% vacancy in LA office. The explanation is supply discipline: construction is essentially zero (0.1% of inventory), and developers are focusing on redevelopment and adaptive reuse rather than new speculative builds. When no new space enters the market, negative absorption doesn't push vacancy up nearly as fast as it would otherwise.

**The submarket split is the key insight.** The San Fernando Valley and San Gabriel Valley are both posting positive rent growth while the metro overall is declining 1.0% YOY. Both suburbs benefit from the same structural advantage: strong local demographics, high household density, and low dependence on international tourism. Retail in these submarkets serves everyday needs — grocery, services, discount — rather than luxury or tourist-facing retail that is softer. This directly matches what CBRE and JLL identify nationally as the winning format: neighborhood, service-oriented, suburban.

**The key transaction** is Round1's 160,000 SF commitment at Stonewood Center in Downey — an entertainment/experience tenant filling an anchor-sized space with a high-traffic, experiential concept (JLL LA Retail Q2 2025). This is the LA-specific version of the national pattern: the tenants filling large-format retail are experiential (fitness, entertainment, food) rather than traditional apparel or department stores.

**Outlook (JLL):** Headwinds expected to continue through 2026 with rent growth trailing national averages. But demolition of obsolete space combined with the minimal construction pipeline will prevent availability from spiking. Strategic opportunities in suburban submarkets with strong demographics.

## National Retail: What JLL and CBRE Agree On

The convergence between JLL (Q4 2025) and CBRE (2026 Outlook) on retail is stronger than on any other sector — both firms describe the same structural dynamic from the same direction:

**Supply scarcity is the defining condition.** JLL: vacancies remain near historic lows, new construction continues to fall, reinforcing landlords' pricing power. CBRE: "space availability will remain near historic lows in 2026 with only a slight uptick where big-box closures occur. New construction will remain limited due to financing constraints, high costs and little land availability" (CBRE Retail 2026). This is not two firms speculating — it reflects a decade-long de-development trend that predates COVID and accelerated through the 2020s.

**Format bifurcation is accelerating.** Both firms name the same winners and losers. Winners: grocery-anchored centers, neighborhood and strip centers, service-oriented tenants (restaurants, healthcare/personal services), high-income suburban corridors. Losers: weaker malls, older power centers, non-essential specialty retail. CBRE: "Performance will diverge more sharply by format and location" with grocery-anchored and neighborhood formats "positioned to outperform for both occupancy and rent growth" while "weaker malls and older power centers will continue to lag" (CBRE Retail 2026). JLL describes the same in terms of "expanding retailers" moving quickly on quality space "particularly in grocery-anchored, neighborhood, and service-oriented formats" (JLL US Retail Q4 2025).

**The consumer driving this:** CBRE explicitly names the behavioral shift — "consumers are becoming more discerning, prioritizing value, essentials, experiences and convenience" with "squeezed discretionary budgets" constraining non-essential specialty retailers (CBRE Retail 2026). Grocery, discount, off-price, quick-service restaurants, and healthcare services are expanding because they serve needs that don't compress with consumer belt-tightening. Specialty retail contracts for the same reason.

**Institutional capital returned to retail.** JLL notes institutional capital surged back in 2025 (JLL US Retail Q4 2025); CBRE notes investment volumes above long-term averages in late 2025 (JLL Global Perspective). This is significant because institutional capital largely exited retail from 2017–2022 during the "retail apocalypse" narrative. The re-entry confirms that the surviving format — neighborhood/grocery/service — has proved its durability.

## National Multifamily: Q1 2026 Update

CBRE's Q1 2026 US Multifamily Figures (published April 28, 2026) provide the most current hard data on national multifamily performance:

| Metric | Q1 2026 Value | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Overall vacancy rate | 4.8% | -20 bps from Q4 2025 |
| Average monthly rent | $2,217 | +0.2% YOY, +0.4% QOQ |
| Net absorption | 78,100 units | Down from 120K units in Q1 2025 |
| Construction completions | 58,100 units | -30% YOY |
| Multifamily investment volume | $29.5B | -6% YOY |

Source: CBRE Q1 2026 US Multifamily Figures (scraped 2026-05-04)

Vacancy fell 20 bps from Q4 2025 back to 4.8% — the first quarter where net absorption outpaced construction completions in three quarters. This confirms the absorption trajectory is moving in the right direction, though the market remains above the 4.4% level recorded in Q3 2025 (before the supply-demand gap widened through late 2025). 63 of 69 markets tracked recorded positive net absorption in Q1. Construction completions of 58,100 units represent a 30% decline YOY and are expected to fall further in coming quarters as the delivery pipeline thins.

The investment picture is softer: Q1 multifamily volume of $29.5B is down 6% YOY, with portfolio sales especially weak (-23% to $3.9B) while individual asset sales held up better (-2.7% to $25.6B). This divergence suggests institutional buyers remain cautious about portfolio-level bets while single-asset buyers are active.

## National Multifamily: The Supply Overhang Story

CBRE's 2026 multifamily outlook is structurally positive long-term but operationally challenged near-term — a distinction worth unpacking.

**The structural case for multifamily demand is strong.** There is a 105% monthly premium to own vs. rent nationally (CBRE Multifamily 2026). An estimated 3.4 million single-family homes are missing from supply. More than half of the $13 trillion in outstanding mortgages is financed at below 4%, meaning existing homeowners won't sell — which keeps existing renters renewing rather than buying. In LA specifically (not from CBRE's LA data, but inferrable from housing market conditions), homeownership costs are among the highest in the country — the buy-vs-rent premium almost certainly exceeds the national 105% figure, meaning LA renters are especially locked into rentals.

**The near-term challenge is a 50-year-high supply wave.** The Sun Belt and Mountain markets — Atlanta, Austin, Denver, Charlotte — absorbed most of the 2021–2024 construction boom. Current multifamily vacancy nationally is 4.4%, below the 2010–2019 average of 5.2%, but new supply is still being absorbed in high-growth markets (CBRE Multifamily 2026). In Austin and Denver, asking rent growth is projected negative in 2026. The national picture is therefore split: coastal, supply-constrained markets (LA, NYC, Boston) are experiencing different conditions than Sun Belt markets.

**Operators are choosing occupancy over rent.** Nationally, multifamily operators are offering concessions to new tenants rather than pushing asking rents — "strategically focused on preserving occupancies" with 57% of all leasing activity now renewals, up from 48% in 2005 (CBRE Multifamily 2026). This creates a gap between headline asking rent figures (which show little or no growth) and blended rent growth (which combines renewals at better rates with new leases at discounted rates). Blended growth is consistently higher — an important nuance for any investor underwriting multifamily in 2026.

**Investment outlook:** Cap rates are expected to remain stable in 2026, with incremental compression in following years as rates stabilize further. Rent control initiatives in Boston, Denver, NYC, and Seattle introduce political risk for investors in those markets; LA has its own existing rent control framework which is a known factor priced into local valuations.

## What This Means for a JLL Analyst

**Retail:** When a client asks about LA retail investment, the defensible answer is format-first, not market-first. The question is not "is LA retail healthy" but "which LA retail format?" Grocery-anchored and neighborhood strip centers in LA's dense suburban corridors (San Fernando Valley, SGV, South Bay) follow the same supply-scarcity logic as the national story — low new construction, essential tenant mix, stable occupancy. Legacy mall assets (e.g., older power centers in less-affluent submarkets) face the same structural headwinds nationally. JLL's institutional capital re-entry signal confirms the former is a buyer's market for quality assets.

**Multifamily:** LA's housing affordability crisis means the structural demand drivers (homeownership barriers, housing shortage) are more acute locally than nationally. The near-term risk — supply overhang — is concentrated in Sun Belt markets, not LA. The operative risk for LA multifamily is rent control framework uncertainty, not supply. This is not in the knowledge base's scraped sources but is known market context.
