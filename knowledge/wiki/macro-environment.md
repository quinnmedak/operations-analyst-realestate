# Macro Environment & Demand Drivers

**Sources synthesized:** CBRE U.S. Market Outlook 2026, JLL U.S. Office Market Dynamics Q1 2026, JLL Global Real Estate Perspective Feb 2026, Bisnow LA Capital Markets Rebound (Feb 2026)

---

## 1. Interest Rates & CRE Valuations

The clearest causal chain in the current CRE cycle runs through interest rates. The 2022–2023 Federal Reserve rate hike cycle drove the sharpest CRE valuation reset in a generation: as debt became expensive, deal volume collapsed, buyers and sellers couldn't agree on price, and transaction markets froze. Los Angeles felt this acutely — sales volume dropped from $51.4B in 2019 to $33B by the end of 2024 (MSCI via Bisnow LA Capital Markets, Feb 2026).

The turn came in 2025. As rates eased, debt became serviceable again, and both buyers and sellers reset their expectations. Colliers Vice Chair Sean Fulp described the shift plainly: "Some of that was linked to a decrease in interest rates in 2025. The market had gotten comfortable and found a way to make sense of real estate values based upon flattening fundamentals and also flattening capital markets" (Bisnow LA Capital Markets, Feb 2026). LA deal volume recovered to $37.3B in 2025 — not a full rebound to pre-pandemic levels, but a meaningful reversal of the slide.

The macro transmission mechanism is direct: lower rates → lower cap rates implied → debt serviceable at today's values → deal volume recovers. CBRE projects cap rates to compress a further 5–15 basis points across most property types in 2026, which would extend the recovery if realized (CBRE U.S. Market Outlook 2026).

## 2. Employment & Office Demand

Office demand is structurally linked to employment in office-using sectors — professional services, finance, legal, tech. When those sectors add jobs, leasing follows. When they shed jobs or slow hiring, leasing contracts. This is not a new relationship, but the post-pandemic period made it messier: some companies leased less space per employee (densification), others leased more to accommodate hybrid schedules (de-densification), and the net effect varied by market.

At the national level, the trend is positive in Q1 2026. JLL reports leasing activity grew 7.6% relative to Q1 2025 and net absorption remained positive for the third consecutive quarter, with 3.5 million SF of occupancy gains nationally. San Francisco (+1.6M SF) and New York (+1.5M SF) drove those gains — markets with large concentrations of financial and tech employment (JLL U.S. Office Q1 2026). Over 50% of organizations globally are now requiring 3–4 days in-office, and JLL's Global Perspective notes that companies are moving into expansion mode (JLL Global Real Estate Perspective, Feb 2026).

LA is recovering but lagging these leaders. The LA Office wiki page documents the specifics: office-using sectors in LA shed jobs YoY through Q2 2025, even as leasing activity recovered. The 14M+ SF leasing total for full-year 2025 was a bright spot, but it came despite employment headwinds — suggesting it was driven more by lease renewals, consolidations, and flight-to-quality moves than by new space demand from net hiring.

## 3. E-Commerce & Industrial Demand

Industrial real estate's outperformance since 2020 has a clear structural driver: the permanent increase in e-commerce share of total retail sales. As more purchasing moved online, the physical infrastructure of distribution — warehouses, fulfillment centers, last-mile hubs — became more valuable. Demand surged, vacancy fell, and rents rose sharply in 2021–2022 in major logistics markets.

That initial surge has moderated, but the structural demand remains. CBRE projects industrial leasing volume to improve slightly in 2026, driven by two forces: reshoring of manufacturing operations, and outsourcing of distribution to third-party logistics (3PL) providers (CBRE U.S. Market Outlook 2026). JLL's Global Perspective adds regionalization of manufacturing and distribution, defense spending, and rising e-commerce as demand supports for 2026 (JLL Global Real Estate Perspective, Feb 2026). These are long-cycle structural shifts — not cyclical bounces — which is why institutional capital has remained more committed to industrial than to office.

In LA specifically, e-commerce demand materialized concretely: Amazon's 615K SF lease in Commerce/Central LA was the single deal that pushed that submarket into positive net absorption and signaled to investors that LA industrial was worth buying (Bisnow LA Capital Markets, Feb 2026). South Bay remains the most active LA industrial submarket, anchored by aerospace, defense, and logistics users rather than pure e-commerce — a differentiated demand base that has kept that submarket's rents ticking upward.

## 4. CRE Loan Stress

Delinquency rates are a lagging indicator of the rate hike cycle's damage. JLL's Q1 2026 data shows CRE loan delinquency rates rose by 74 basis points to 11.41% from December to March — a continued upward trend even as deal markets recover (JLL U.S. Office Q1 2026). This creates a bifurcated picture: transaction markets are reviving while distressed loan workouts are simultaneously playing out in the background.

The investment implication is counterintuitive: buildings that traded at a reset basis in 2023–2025 are now seeing steady occupancy gains under new ownership (JLL U.S. Office Q1 2026). Distressed-asset buyers who bought at discounted prices are benefiting from forced repricing that the original owners couldn't absorb. The Sunset Media Center sale ($61M at 51% occupancy) and 601 S. Figueroa repositioning in LA both fit this pattern — detailed in the Capital Markets wiki page. Rising delinquencies don't necessarily signal broader market collapse; they signal ongoing price discovery and the gradual clearing of overleveraged legacy positions.

## 5. 2026 Macro Outlook

CBRE and JLL broadly agree on the macro setup for 2026: slowing but still-positive GDP growth, stabilizing interest rates, and a rebound in CRE investment that is large on a percentage basis because the 2023–2024 base was so depressed.

CBRE's headline: GDP growth slows to 2.0% in 2026 with marginally lower inflation averaging 2.5%; CRE investment increases 16% to $562B, nearly matching the 2015–2019 pre-pandemic annual average (CBRE U.S. Market Outlook 2026). JLL's Global Perspective shows global CRE investment volumes were already up 19% in 2025 vs. 2024, with cross-border investment up 25% — recovery is underway, not just projected (JLL Global Real Estate Perspective, Feb 2026).

The two meaningful risks to this outlook are interest rate volatility (CBRE notes financial markets will remain volatile due to government and economic policy, particularly trade) and the ongoing delinquency cycle for legacy loans. Neither is expected to derail the recovery, but both could slow it. For LA specifically, cross-border investment has dried up in 2026 year-to-date — a data point flagged in the Capital Markets wiki page — while private capital has filled the gap.

The macro signal an analyst should watch: the rate environment and employment in office-using sectors are the two leading indicators for CRE leasing and investment activity. When both point in the same direction, the market follows with a lag of roughly two to four quarters.
