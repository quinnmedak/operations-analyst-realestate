# National CRE Trends

**Last updated:** April 2026  
**Primary sources:** JLL US Office Q1 2026, JLL US Industrial Q1 2026, JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026, CBRE US Market Outlook 2026, C&W Greater LA MarketBeat

---

## Sector Scoreboard

### Office — Recovering, but bifurcated

National office is in recovery mode but the aggregate number masks a structural split. JLL reports positive net absorption for the third consecutive quarter in Q1 2026 (+3.5M SF), leasing up 7.6% YOY, and same-asset rents up 0.8% in the past year (JLL US Office Q1 2026). The pipeline is at its lowest recorded level — 22.3M SF under construction — and total inventory has declined 25M SF from its 2023 peak as conversions and demolitions remove secondary product. Single-asset sales volume hit $11.5B in Q1, highest since 2020.

The headline recovery is almost entirely a prime-space story. C&W national reports Class A net absorption of +1.4M SF in the most recent quarter, with a four-quarter rolling total of +18.7M SF — all prime. CBRE projects "even more scarcity of available prime space by year-end 2026" with spillover demand to the next tier only in early-recovery markets (CBRE 2026 Outlook). Meanwhile, delinquency rates are rising — up 74 bps to 11.41% from December to March 2026 — concentrated in secondary buildings and markets that never attracted post-pandemic leasing (JLL US Office Q1 2026). Two recoveries happening simultaneously: prime recovering, secondary deteriorating further.

### Industrial — Healthy, flight to quality within the sector

National industrial vacancy held at 7.5% in Q1 2026 and is expected to begin declining as supply absorption continues and new construction starts remain flat (JLL US Industrial Q1 2026). Leasing volume hit 145.2M SF in Q1 (+17.8% YOY), with 71.6% being new leases rather than renewals — a commitment signal. Big-box leasing (500K SF+) surged 80.7% YOY, reversing the cautious approach of 2023–2024. Net absorption of 50.9M SF was "exceptional strength for the period" per JLL.

CBRE frames the industrial recovery as a "flight to quality" story: occupiers are consolidating into more efficient, modern facilities at the expense of older assets — exactly what the C&W three-quarter LA data shows in the SGV and Central LA submarkets (CBRE 2026 Outlook). JLL's Global Perspective notes logistics leasing rebounded in both North America and Europe in 2025, supported by regionalization of manufacturing/distribution, defense spending, and e-commerce (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026).

### Retail — Resilient, format-driven divergence

National retail vacancy is near historic lows with positive net absorption returning in H2 2025 and institutional capital surging back in (JLL US Retail Q4 2025). The supply pipeline is essentially empty — financing constraints, high construction costs, and land scarcity mean virtually no new speculative retail is being built nationally. This supply discipline is the primary reason vacancy hasn't risen despite negative absorption in softer markets.

CBRE identifies the winning tenants explicitly: grocery, discount, off-price, quick-service restaurants, and service retailers — all formats that serve essential or convenience needs. The losers are non-essential specialty retailers facing "squeezed discretionary budgets" and discretionary big-box formats (CBRE 2026 Outlook). This is not a market-wide story; it is a format rotation.

### Multifamily — Stable demand, supply absorption underway

National multifamily vacancy is 4.4%, below the 2010–2019 average of 5.2%, but a 50-year-high supply wave concentrated in the Sun Belt is forcing operators to prioritize occupancy over rent growth (CBRE 2026 Outlook). The structural demand case is strong — 105% monthly premium to buy vs. rent, 3.4 million single-family homes short nationally — but near-term rent growth is muted as excess Sun Belt units clear. Global investment volumes for the living sector rose 24% in 2025 (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026).

### Life Sciences — Oversupplied, long recovery horizon

61 million SF of lab space is currently available nationally, with an availability rate of approximately 29% — up 20.4 percentage points in three years (JLL Life Sciences Outlook / Lab Market Newsroom). Recovery is structural, not cyclical: AI-native biotechs lease roughly one-third less space than traditional tenants, and venture capital's share of biotech funding has fallen from 15% historically to 7% recently. JLL projects availability declining to ~20% by 2030 through supply exits and adaptive reuse, not demand recovery (JLL Lab Market Newsroom). This is the only sector where a decade-long structural adjustment is underway.

## Office vs. Industrial Divergence — Cross-Sector Synthesis

The office/industrial gap is the defining cross-sector story of 2025–2026. The two sectors share a Property Clock phase ("Bottoming out" in LA) but for structurally different reasons that produce different investment implications.

**Three layers explain the divergence:**

**Structural demand moved permanently in opposite directions.** E-commerce permanently expanded the physical distribution infrastructure required to serve online purchasing — demand that doesn't reverse in downturns. Meanwhile, hybrid work reduced the office space required per knowledge worker. LA's office-using sectors shed jobs YoY through mid-2025 (financial activities -4,700, professional services -4,100) even as LA industrial logged its highest leasing pace since 2021 (Cushman Q3 2025 / LA Office wiki). These sectors are not on different points of the same cycle; they are on structurally different trajectories.

**Supply dynamics are mirror images.** Industrial supply is structurally constrained — LA's infill nature caps spec construction at ~1.2% of inventory, the pipeline is tapering, and nationally new starts remain flat. Office has the opposite problem: 24.1% vacancy in LA across 212M SF, and nationally the pipeline is low only because nobody is financing new construction into a market already 14%+ vacant. Industrial is tight because demand absorbed the surplus. Office is "low new supply" because the existing glut is still being cleared (LA Office wiki / LA Industrial wiki).

**Capital is differentiating by sector.** REIT share of SoCal industrial acquisitions collapsed from 24.7% to 7.7% in Q4 2025 — not because industrial fundamentals deteriorated, but because private capital moved faster on the bottom call and REITs' cost of capital made acquisitions difficult at current pricing. In office, CBRE's sentiment survey puts office last of five sectors for 10-year performance but notes the first uptick in bullish respondents (14% now expect office to be the best performer, up from near-zero) — the floor is forming, but tentatively (Capital Markets wiki).

**For REIT investors, the divergence shows up in stock vs. fundamentals gaps.** Industrial REITs (Prologis, Rexford, EastGroup) trade at premiums that price in structural demand tailwinds; the risk is stocks running ahead of still-correcting underlying fundamentals (LA industrial Q4 2025 absorption reverted to -116K SF after Q3's positive reading). Office REITs (Kilroy, Hudson Pacific) trade at discounts pricing in structural decline; Hudson Pacific's forced sale of its Element LA campus to Riot Games at reset pricing is the concrete expression of that stress. Whether the 14M SF 2025 LA office leasing total proves durable or was a one-year anomaly is the key question for office REIT investors.

## Where JLL, CBRE, and C&W Agree

Three areas of clear convergence across all three research firms — meaning the conclusions are more credible than any single-firm view:

**1. The office recovery is prime-only.** JLL's positive absorption figures, CBRE's "scarcity of prime space" forecast, and C&W's +18.7M SF rolling Class A absorption all describe the same market: strong demand for the top 10–15% of office product, ongoing deterioration in everything else. No firm projects a broad-market office recovery before 2027 at the earliest.

**2. Format bifurcation in retail is accelerating.** JLL, CBRE, and JLL Global Perspective all name the same retail formats as winners (grocery-anchored, neighborhood, service-oriented) and losers (weaker malls, power centers, specialty retail). The mechanism — supply scarcity plus essential-demand resilience — is described identically across firms.

**3. Industrial fundamentals are solid, 3PL is driving it.** JLL's Tenant Demand Study (+12.8% 3PL demand, -16.7% traditional retail), CBRE's "reshoring and 3PL outsourcing" framing, and JLL Global Perspective's logistics rebound language all point to the same structural demand story. No firm expresses concern about national industrial oversupply; the LA situation (post-pandemic spec correction) is a local story, not a national one.

## Where They Diverge

The differences are mostly tonal rather than substantive:

**Macro caution:** CBRE explicitly forecasts GDP slowing to 2.0% in 2026 with "softening labor market conditions" (CBRE 2026 Outlook). JLL Global Perspective frames 2026 as "steady economic growth supported by lower interest rates, contained inflation and increasing fiscal spending" — more optimistic framing of the same environment. Both project investment recovery; CBRE puts a specific number on it ($562B, +16%).

**Office delinquency framing:** JLL calls out delinquency rising to 11.41% as a notable risk (JLL US Office Q1 2026). CBRE's outlook doesn't emphasize delinquency, focusing instead on the prime/secondary bifurcation. Same underlying reality, different emphasis.

LA retail vacancy at 5.8% is in line with the national "near historic lows" characterization.

## JLL Property Clock — Q4 2025

The Property Clock places markets on a cycle from "Rents falling" through "Bottoming out" → "Rents rising" → "Growth accelerating."

**Bottoming out (office and industrial):** Los Angeles, Boston, San Francisco, Chicago, Washington DC, Hong Kong, Beijing, Shanghai  
**Growth accelerating:** Dallas, New York, Mumbai, Tokyo, Madrid, Dubai, Delhi, Milan, Paris, Stockholm, Seoul, Berlin, Frankfurt, London, Warsaw

Source: JLL Global Real Estate Perspective Feb 2026

LA's position — same phase as Boston and Chicago, behind New York and Dallas — reflects a market that has passed the worst of its correction but has not yet transitioned to active recovery. The cross-border comparison is instructive: LA is in the same cycle phase as other gateway cities that experienced deep post-pandemic corrections (SF, DC), while markets with stronger domestic demand inflows (Dallas, Miami) recovered faster.

## 2026 Outlook

**Investment:** CBRE projects $562B in US CRE investment in 2026 (+16%), nearly matching the 2015–2019 pre-pandemic average. Cap rates expected to compress 5–15 bps across most property types. Total returns will be income-driven rather than appreciation-driven (CBRE 2026 Outlook). JLL's global data shows investment volumes up 19% in 2025, with cross-border investment up 25% — the international capital return that typically precedes broader market recovery (JLL Global Perspective Feb 2026).

**Leasing:** Office recovery continues in prime product; industrial consolidation into efficient space; retail format leaders expand while laggards contract; multifamily demand stable as homeownership barriers persist. No sector is projecting a dramatic swing in either direction — this is a recovery characterized by gradual normalization, not a rebound.
