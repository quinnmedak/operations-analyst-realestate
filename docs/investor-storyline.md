# Investor Client Storyline

How to walk an investor client through the dashboard — descriptive, diagnostic, recommendation.

---

## Descriptive — What Happened

Chart 1 is your opening. You tell the client: since 2018, industrial real estate has nearly tripled in investor value while office has lost a third. This is not noise — the gap opened in 2020 and has widened every year since. The KPI row puts the physical market behind it: office space is 24.1% empty with 12 consecutive quarters of tenants giving back more space than they're taking. Industrial sits at 4.8% vacant with absorption turning positive for the first time since 2022. Two sectors, same city, completely opposite stories.

---

## Diagnostic — Why

Chart 2 explains office. The Fed raised rates from near zero to 5.33% in 18 months — the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years. Office got hit from both sides simultaneously: higher rates raised the cost to finance a building purchase (shrinking the buyer pool) and raised refinancing costs for existing owners (putting them under financial stress). The chart shows office prices falling in near-lockstep with the rate hikes. That's not coincidence.

Chart 5 explains why industrial didn't follow. E-commerce grew from roughly 11% to 16% of all retail sales through COVID and stayed there. That created permanent demand for warehouses and logistics space that doesn't go away when rates rise or the economy slows. Industrial landlords never needed tenants the way office landlords do.

Chart 6 answers the question every client asks: is this 2008? No. Current delinquency is around 1.8% — less than one-quarter of the 8.7% peak in 2010. The lending market is under pressure, not in crisis. Distressed sellers exist, but the system isn't frozen.

---

## Recommendation — What to Do

That depends on what the client is solving for.

**Value-add at distressed pricing → office is the play.** Landlords who bought at 2021 valuations using 2021 debt are now underwater on both the asset value and the refinancing cost. They will sign whatever lease it takes to generate cash flow. A well-capitalized buyer can acquire at reset pricing and wait for the rate cut cycle to compress cap rates and restore value. The risk is that remote work is a structural demand reduction, not a cyclical one — if tenants never come back, there is no recovery to wait for. Chart 4 (employment in office-using sectors) is the forward indicator: LA is lagging Dallas and New York, which means don't underwrite a fast recovery.

**Durable income with lower risk → industrial is the better hold.** The demand driver is structural, vacancy is low, and rents are still above pre-COVID levels. The problem is entry price — after a 160% run, you are paying for a lot of future performance upfront. The better industrial opportunity is in submarkets like LA South where spec deliveries have temporarily softened absorption, creating a discount to the broader market.

**The swing factor is the rate cut cycle.** If rates fall meaningfully in the next 12–18 months, cap rates compress, office values recover, and today's distressed buyer looks prescient. If rates stay elevated, the structural demand problem from remote work becomes the dominant story and recovery extends for years. That is the bet the client is actually making.
