## Q1: What is the current state of the LA industrial market?

The LA industrial market has confirmed a recovery through three quarters of 2025 data. After 10 consecutive quarters of negative absorption, YTD net absorption turned positive in Q3 2025 (+1.6M SF) — the first positive reading since 2022. Vacancy held at 4.8%, well below the national 7.5%. Full-year 2025 leasing hit ~33.3M SF, the highest in four years. The main risk is LA South, where spec deliveries in Long Beach/Compton have kept that submarket's absorption negative (-1.8M SF YTD Q3), partially offsetting the SGV and LA Central recovery. Cushman & Wakefield explicitly forecast rents "bottoming out soon" as of Q3 2025.

*Sources: [LA Industrial Market](../knowledge/wiki/la-industrial-market.md), [National CRE Trends](../knowledge/wiki/national-cre-trends.md)*

## Q2: Is LA office recovering or still declining?

It's neither a clean recovery nor a continuing decline — the market passed a mid-2025 trough and partially rebounded. The headline 24.1% vacancy and 12 consecutive quarters of negative net absorption are hard facts. But Q4 2025 availability fell 60 bps to 27.6%, full-year 2025 leasing hit 14M+ SF (a post-pandemic high), and JLL's Property Clock places LA in the "Bottoming out" phase as of Q4 2025. The trophy/commodity bifurcation is the key structural dynamic: LA West is the only submarket with positive YTD net absorption, while DTLA sits at 32%+ vacancy across 39M SF. No source projects a sharp recovery — this is gradual stabilization, not a rebound.

*Sources: [LA Office Market](../knowledge/wiki/la-office-market.md), [National CRE Trends](../knowledge/wiki/national-cre-trends.md)*

## Q3: How do rising interest rates affect CRE valuations?

The causal chain is direct: higher rates → higher cap rates → lower asset values → deal volume collapses. The 2022–2023 Fed rate hike cycle drove LA sales volume from $51.4B (2019) to $33B by end of 2024. As rates eased in 2025, debt became serviceable again, buyer and seller expectations reset, and LA volume recovered to $37.3B. CBRE's H1 2025 Cap Rate Survey confirmed cap rates peaked and are now compressing — projecting a further 5–15 bps of compression in 2026. The inverse relationship runs symmetrically: rate decreases compress cap rates, increase valuations, and unlock deal flow. CRE loan delinquency at 11.41% (up 74 bps, Q1 2026) is the lagging damage from the rate hike cycle still working through the system.

*Sources: [Macro Environment](../knowledge/wiki/macro-environment.md), [Capital Markets](../knowledge/wiki/capital-markets.md)*

## Q4: What sectors are analysts most bullish on for 2026?

Across JLL, CBRE, and Cushman & Wakefield:

- **Industrial** — consensus bullish. Big-box leasing up 80.7% YOY in Q1 2026, 3PL demand up 12.8% nationally, vacancy declining. All three firms agree on the structural demand story (reshoring, e-commerce, 3PL outsourcing).
- **Retail (select formats)** — grocery-anchored, neighborhood, and service-oriented retail is where all three firms converge. Supply scarcity plus essential-demand resilience. CBRE and JLL describe identical format winners/losers.
- **Multifamily** — ranked #1 for long-term investment performance by CBRE H2 2025 survey respondents. Structural demand intact (105% monthly ownership premium, 3.4M SF housing shortage); near-term rent growth muted in Sun Belt supply-saturated markets.
- **Office** — ranked last by CBRE respondents but improving for the first time: 14% now see it as best 10-year performer, up from near-zero.

*Sources: [National CRE Trends](../knowledge/wiki/national-cre-trends.md), [Capital Markets](../knowledge/wiki/capital-markets.md)*

## Q5: What's driving investment activity in LA right now?

Three forces:

1. **Rate normalization** — rates easing made debt serviceable again and unlocked buyer/seller price agreement, driving LA volume from $33B (2024) to $37.3B (2025).
2. **Private capital dominance** — 61.4% of LA buyers are private capital, the highest share in a decade. Private buyers are moving on conviction that fundamentals have bottomed; institutional buyers are waiting for "a couple years of good returns" before allocating.
3. **Value-add at reset pricing** — the dominant thesis. Sunset Media Center at $61M (51% occupied, ~$187/SF), 601 S. Figueroa at $210M (repositioning after Brookfield stress), and owner-users buying office and industrial at below-peak valuations (Riot Games $150M + $81M lease termination). Cross-border buying has dried up YTD 2026 due to policy uncertainty.

*Sources: [Capital Markets](../knowledge/wiki/capital-markets.md), [Macro Environment](../knowledge/wiki/macro-environment.md), [LA Office Market](../knowledge/wiki/la-office-market.md)*

## Q6: How does LA's office market compare to other major cities?

| Market | Vacancy | Property Clock Phase |
|---|---|---|
| Los Angeles | ~27.6% availability (Q4 2025) | Bottoming out |
| San Francisco | 34.4% | Bottoming out |
| New York | 14.1% | Growth accelerating |
| Dallas | 25.7% | Growth accelerating |
| Washington DC | 23.0% | Bottoming out |

LA is better than San Francisco (which reflects more severe tech industry contraction), worse than New York (anchored by financial services employment), and in the same "Bottoming out" phase as Boston, Chicago, and DC. Dallas has higher vacancy than LA but is in "Growth accelerating" due to strong population/employment inflows. Nationally, Q1 2026 positive absorption was driven by NYC (+1.5M SF) and SF (+1.6M SF) — LA is not yet contributing to national positive absorption figures.

*Sources: [LA Office Market](../knowledge/wiki/la-office-market.md), [National CRE Trends](../knowledge/wiki/national-cre-trends.md)*

## Q7: What is the investment story for life sciences real estate?

It's primarily a distressed-asset and selective-recovery story. 61M SF of lab space is available nationally at ~29% availability — the highest in at least a decade, up 20.4 percentage points in three years. The oversupply came from a pandemic-era construction boom that outpaced demand, compounded by two structural headwinds: AI-native biotechs leasing one-third less space than traditional tenants, and VC biotech funding falling from 15% to 7% of total VC. JLL projects availability declining to ~20% by 2030, but through supply exits and adaptive reuse of 18.7M SF — not demand recovery.

For LA specifically: the city ranks 3rd nationally in AI life sciences integration (behind Bay Area and Boston), which positions it for the next cycle of growth if AI-native biotechs drive the recovery. The investment entry point is distressed pricing on well-located lab product in LA's emerging clusters (Century City, El Segundo, Torrance) — but those tenants lease smaller footprints, so per-SF occupancy metrics may recover before overall absorption does.

*Sources: [Life Sciences](../knowledge/wiki/life-sciences.md), [National CRE Trends](../knowledge/wiki/national-cre-trends.md)*
